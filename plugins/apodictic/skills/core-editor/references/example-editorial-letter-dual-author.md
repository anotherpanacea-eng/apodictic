### Maya Okonkwo | 78,000 words | Complete draft
*APODICTIC Development Editor — a developmental read of your manuscript.*

## The Short Version

You've written a dual-timeline literary novel with a genuinely strong voice and a close that
earns its restraint. The book's main problem is structural and concentrated: the middle third
compresses its emotional aftermath into summary, so the weight of the central choice never
fully lands. This is targeted revision, not a reconception — the voice and the architecture
are keepers.

## What the Book Does Best

The dual-POV voice holds across both timelines, and the close earns its restraint. The
sister-relationship arc carries the emotional spine without ever stating it outright — protect
this in revision.

## What Needs Work

- **Must-Fix:** Pacing collapse in the middle third. The aftermath compression recurs in
  Chapter 7 (lines 142-160) and again in Chapter 9 (line 220), where three days pass in two
  sentences and the consequence of the central choice is summarized rather than dramatized.
- **Should-Fix:** The prologue's frame competes with Chapter 1 for the reader's first
  orientation (Chapter 1, lines 1-40).

## Revision Checklist

- Dramatize the aftermath of the Chapter 7 choice on-page rather than summarizing it.
- Add the missing causal beat at the Chapter 9 turn (line 220) so the consequence follows the
  choice on the page.
- Fold the prologue's one load-bearing detail into Chapter 1, then cut the prologue.
- Decide whether Chapter 5 belongs in Part I or Part II — the choice changes the middle-third
  pacing plan.

## Protected Elements

- Voice consistency across Part I — the most load-bearing strength; don't let revision pressure
  on the middle third flatten it.
- The dual-POV architecture between Maya and Jonah.
- The sister-relationship arc through the close.
- The final image of Chapter 12.

## Author Decisions

### Keep

- Keep the dual POV.
- Keep the unreliable-narrator frame.

### Cut

- The prologue: fold its one load-bearing detail into Chapter 1, then let it go.

### Unsure

- Unsure whether Chapter 5 stays in Part I or moves to Part II — the decision changes the
  middle-third pacing plan.

## Control Questions

1. What does the protagonist learn in the final third?
2. Whose POV closes Part II?
3. Does the prologue earn its place, or is its one detail recoverable in Chapter 1?
4. What is the cost of Chapter 7's choice, and is it paid on-page?
5. Is Chapter 5 working in its current position?
6. Does the final image land as the close intends?
7. What is the book's controlling idea?

## The Strongest Case Against

The middle third asks the reader to feel the weight of a choice the book never dramatizes, and
a confident voice is doing the work that structure should. The risk is a reader who finishes
Part I engaged and drifts through the middle before the close recovers them.
