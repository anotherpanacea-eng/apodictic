## What This Document Does

This document translates the level-setting research into a compact prompt-family inventory. It is **not** a live module spec and **not** a writer-facing prompt workbook.

Its purpose is to help a future APODICTIC implementation answer four questions:

1. which prompt family fits this diagnosed blockage?
2. what structural work is the family supposed to do?
3. what would make the prompt drift across the firewall?
4. when should the coach suppress prompting entirely?

---

## Use Discipline

Before any future prompt is selected, the coach should confirm:

1. the block has been diagnosed
2. the relevant corridor in `Diagnostic_State.md` or `Argument_State.md` is known
3. a prompt is preferable to reframe, rest, cutting, research, or reassessment
4. only one primary family is being used at a time

If those conditions are not met, the right answer is usually **no prompt**.

## Prompt-Family Matrix

| Family | Target block type(s) | Target root-cause / state pattern | Structural purpose | Allowed constraint logic | Forbidden / prescriptive drift signs | Sample prompt skeleton | Delivery fit | Genre / argument notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Constraint** | Cognitive overload; decisional; stage-mismatch; partial structural uncertainty | Agency collapse, unstable turning point, blurred claim corridor, objection avoidance, testimony bleed | Force one structural requirement to become explicit while freezing other variables | May specify who must initiate, which layer must remain fixed, which distinction must hold, or which burden must be met | Naming the event, revelation, policy conclusion, relationship outcome, or paragraph content | `Using [corridor], draft 2-3 variants where [structural requirement] is true while [other variable] stays fixed. Then test which version best satisfies [diagnostic condition].` | Session plan; stuck-point; partial-manuscript; argument coaching | Best default family. In argument work, use for claim paths, warrant bridges, or testimony containment rather than sentence drafting. |
| **Inversion** | Cognitive; motivational-avoidance; decisional | Habitual reactive scenes, repeatedly deferred objection, safe but inert claim order, fear-driven repetition | Expose the current default by forcing its opposite | May require opposite structural motion: initiate instead of react, concede earlier instead of later, show consequence instead of explanation | Dictating the exact opposite event, naming the revelation, or implying that the inverted version is the final answer | `Draft one version in which the draft's habitual move is reversed: instead of [current structural habit], the section must [opposite structural move]. Compare what changes downstream.` | Stuck-point; session plan; argument coaching | Particularly useful when the writer keeps reproducing the same safe move. In horror or thriller, inversion must still respect contract pressure. |
| **Isolation** | Cognitive overload; aesthetic-execution gap; accumulated complexity | Too many moving parts, mixed scene functions, muddled warrants, tone-content entanglement | Separate one layer of work from the others so it can be tested alone | May isolate emotion from plot, plot from exposition, claim from evidence, observation from interpretation | Asking for a finished replacement scene or paragraph instead of an isolated layer | `Work only on [single layer] in [corridor]. Ignore [other layers] for now. When done, check whether the isolated layer now makes the next revision decision clearer.` | Session plan; stuck-point; partial-manuscript; argument coaching | High-value family for `Argument_State.md` work because it respects dependency order. Also useful for tonal drift and mixed-function scenes. |
| **Scale-shift** | Cognitive; stage-mismatch; pacing or compression problems | Scene sprawl, overbuilt section, diffuse op-ed, bloated objection handling, summary where dramatization is needed | Change the size of the unit so the load-bearing material becomes visible | May compress to one paragraph / six sentences / one claim ladder, or expand one line into a full structural unit | Requiring final polish in the compressed version, or dictating exactly which sentence/claim must remain | `Compress [corridor] to [small unit]. Then expand only the part that still carries the structural load. What turned out to be necessary?` | Session plan; compression pass; argument coaching | Excellent for pacing, compression, and opening/closing problems. In argument work, use to test whether concession logic or claim sequence survives shorter form. |
| **Perspective** | Cognitive; identity/vision threat; objection pressure | Motivation blind spot, antagonist opacity, audience mismatch, hostile-reader vulnerability, witness overreach | Reveal what the current vantage cannot see | May switch to another legitimate vantage, reader posture, or witness position while keeping the same structural corridor | Naming what the other character or audience must say, believe, or conclude | `Rework [corridor] from the vantage of [other legitimate perspective / audience]. Focus only on what this position wants, knows, contests, or fears. Then check what this exposes in the original.` | Stuck-point; argument coaching; red-team follow-up; partial-manuscript | Strong in argument revision for objections, audience recalibration, and testimony containment. In fiction, use only perspectives the draft has already earned. |
| **Deletion** | Cognitive; stage-mismatch; overbuild; scope creep | Scene bloat, overexplained stakes, unnecessary subplot matter, verbose warranting, compression needs | Discover what survives when non-load-bearing matter is removed | May require cutting a percentage, keeping only what changes relation / cost / claim, or rebuilding from what remains | Prescribing what must be cut by content, or treating the deletion experiment as the final editorial verdict | `Cut [portion] from [corridor]. Keep only what still changes the structural situation. Then identify what, if anything, must be rebuilt.` | Session plan; compression; partial-manuscript | Best for overbuild and scope creep. Often the right answer is simply to cut, so this family should not be used when a direct cut decision is already obvious and accepted. |
| **Temporal** | Cognitive; partial-manuscript uncertainty; consequence fog | Weak aftermath, unclear setup debt, inert reveal timing, absent downstream cost, unclear testimony consequence | Use time movement to expose commitment, consequence, or missing preparation | May move briefly to immediate aftermath, later memory, prior setup moment, or downstream implication while staying abstract | Naming the future event that must happen, or inventing sequel content as if it were already chosen | `Move to [earlier / later] relative to [corridor] and track only what changed in cost, knowledge, relationship, or burden. Then return and ask what the current draft must earn.` | Partial-manuscript; reveal economy; argument coaching | Especially useful when the writer cannot tell what a scene or claim corridor is obligating. In argument work, temporal prompts are better for consequence chains than for evidence gathering. |

## Firewall Test Table

These examples use placeholders only. They are meant to show the boundary, not to provide finished prompts.

| Domain | Verdict | Example | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fiction | **Pass** | `Draft three versions of [scene corridor] in which the POV character initiates the turn rather than reacting. Which version creates the clearest downstream pressure?` | State-grounded, multi-solution, and self-evaluative |
| Fiction | **Edge case** | `Rewrite [scene] so [character] reveals the secret they have been hiding.` | The structural locus is plausible, but the event is already being chosen for the writer |
| Fiction | **Fail** | `Write the breakup scene where [character A] tells [character B] they are leaving town.` | Event, content, and outcome are prescribed |
| Fiction | **Pass** | `Compress [scene] to one paragraph. Keep only what changes relation, cost, or knowledge. What still matters?` | Pure structural compression test |
| Fiction | **Edge case** | `Write the scene ten years later when they meet again and realize what they lost.` | Temporal move is allowed in principle, but this names a future event and realization |
| Fiction | **Fail** | `Write the epilogue where the family reconciles.` | Fully prescriptive outcome |
| Argument | **Pass** | `Generate three possible warrants connecting [C1] to [C0] while keeping the evidence fixed. Which warrant survives the strongest logged objection?` | Explores bridge logic without scripting claims or wording |
| Argument | **Edge case** | `Draft the counterargument paragraph answering the cost objection with one statistic and one anecdote.` | Better than pure ghostwriting, but still specifies paragraph form and evidence recipe too tightly |
| Argument | **Fail** | `Write a conclusion arguing that the policy will protect children and lower taxes.` | Chooses the claim content and desired outcome |
| Argument | **Pass** | `Separate [testimony corridor] into observation, interpretation, and recommendation. Which layer is currently carrying more burden than it should?` | Structural separation, not witness scripting |

## Scenario Checks

These are the minimum scenario classes the future module should be validated against.

### 1. Fiction structural blockage from `Diagnostic_State.md`

- Best candidates: Constraint, Isolation, Scale-shift, Perspective
- Typical state patterns:
  - agency collapse
  - pacing drag from overbuilt scenes
  - reveal flatness
  - tonal drift
- Suppress prompting when:
  - the issue is a straightforward cut decision already accepted
  - the writer is exhausted rather than blocked

### 2. Partial-manuscript forward-writing blockage

- Best candidates: Constraint, Temporal, Isolation
- Typical state patterns:
  - structural uncertainty
  - character motivation gap
  - fear of the hard next scene
- Use discipline:
  - keep the unit small
  - frame the prompt as exploratory
  - tie it to the next drafting decision, not to the whole book

### 3. Argument revision blockage from `Argument_State.md`

- Best candidates: Isolation, Constraint, Perspective, Scale-shift
- Typical state patterns:
  - blurred claim corridor
  - warrant gap
  - objection avoidance
  - testimony containment
  - audience mismatch
- Suppress prompting when:
  - the next step is evidence acquisition or factual verification
  - the claim path is still too unstable to explore downstream form

### 4. Accumulated-feedback / overmediated case

- Default answer: **no prompt**
- If anything is offered, use only one very low-load prompt, usually Isolation or Deletion
- Preferred alternatives:
  - reread without notes
  - inventory what still works
  - short break
  - outside-reader reset

## Family Validation Matrix

Each family should be screened against the three design tests named in the research spec.

| Family | Diagnostic specificity | Firewall compliance | Cognitive load | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Constraint | Strong | Strong | Conditional | Excellent when the state is sharp; overwhelming if the corridor is too broad |
| Inversion | Medium to strong | Strong | Strong | Best when the draft's habitual move is already visible |
| Isolation | Strong | Strong | Strong | Safest family for complex states and argument work |
| Scale-shift | Strong | Strong | Strong | Usually lowers load quickly |
| Perspective | Medium | Conditional strong | Conditional | High value, but easiest family to let drift into content invention |
| Deletion | Strong | Strong | Strong | Best when the issue is excess, not absence |
| Temporal | Medium | Conditional strong | Conditional | Powerful for consequence fog, risky if it starts inventing future canon |

**Read rule:** If a family rates only "medium" on specificity and "conditional" on load for the current state, the coach should prefer a reframe or no prompt unless the writer explicitly wants an experiment.

## No-Prompt Zones

The future module should explicitly recognize states where the correct intervention is not a prompt.

| State | Why prompting is wrong | Better move |
|---|---|---|
| Physiological depletion / burnout | More structure becomes more pressure | Rest, rescope, lighter task, deadline honesty |
| Clear evidence-acquisition gap | The work is research, not writing | Evidence queue, verification, narrowing claim |
| Accepted cut decision | Discovery is over; execution is straightforward | Cut and propagate consequences |
| Factual or continuity error | The issue is correction, not invention | Fix directly |
| Accumulated-feedback saturation | Another layer of mediation worsens the state | Strip back support, reread, outside reader |
| Locked Keep/Cut decision the writer has not reopened | Prompting would quietly relitigate a settled choice | Respect the lock |

---

## Bottom Line

This inventory is intentionally small. Its argument is that APODICTIC should prefer:

1. a few reusable prompt families
2. strong state-grounding
3. one prompt at a time
4. explicit no-prompt cases

That is the only shape of prompt system that appears compatible with the live Revision Coach, the argument engine, and the firewall.
