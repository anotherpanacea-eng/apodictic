## What exists now

The Revision Coach already handles reactive stuck-point coaching:

- **Three-way block diagnosis** (cognitive / motivational / physiological) with matched interventions
- **Exercise library** (Name the Resistance, Sit with the Draft, Write Toward the Difficulty, Permission Draft, Inventory What Works, Seek Outside Readers)
- **Partial manuscript stall-cause coaching** (structural uncertainty, character motivation gap, tonal drift, scope creep, fear of commitment, exhaustion)
- **Forward-writing session plans** for incomplete drafts

All of this is reactive: the writer says "I'm stuck," the coach diagnoses the block type, and intervenes.

## What doesn't exist

**Proactive, structurally-informed prompt generation.** Given a diagnosed problem (from Diagnostic_State.md or Argument_State.md), generate writing prompts, exercises, or structural experiments that help the writer explore solutions — without prescribing content.

The gap: a writer staring at a diagnosed root cause ("character agency collapses in Act 2") gets a reframe and a decomposition, but no concrete prompt to start writing toward a fix. The coach says "what has this character earned or learned that could drive the turning point?" — which is good coaching, but it's still a question, not a prompt. Some writers need the prompt.

## The Firewall Problem

This is where it gets hard. APODICTIC's core constraint says: diagnose structure, never write content. A prompt that says "write a scene where X happens" crosses the firewall. A prompt that says "draft three versions of the Act 2 turning point where the character initiates rather than reacts" is right on the line — it specifies a structural constraint without prescribing content.

The research needs to establish where structurally-informed prompts live relative to the firewall, and what makes a prompt structural vs. prescriptive.

### Preliminary hypothesis

A prompt is structural when it:
1. Names the diagnosed mechanism (from the diagnostic state)
2. Specifies a constraint the draft must satisfy (from the root cause)
3. Leaves all aesthetic, content, and execution decisions to the writer

A prompt is prescriptive when it:
1. Specifies what should happen in the story
2. Names characters, events, or outcomes
3. Implies a single correct solution

The research should test this distinction against real examples and edge cases.

## Research Questions

### 1. What does the writing pedagogy literature say about structurally-informed prompts?

**Why it matters:** We need theoretical grounding for the distinction between structural prompts and content prescription. The creative writing workshop tradition has a version of this (craft exercises vs. assignments), and composition pedagogy has another (heuristic prompts vs. topic prompts). Neither maps perfectly onto APODICTIC's diagnostic-to-revision pipeline.

**What to look for:**
- Craft exercise design in MFA pedagogy — what makes an exercise generative vs. constraining?
- Constraint-based creativity research (Stokes, 2005; others) — do structural constraints reliably produce more creative output than open prompts?
- The "Oulipo problem" — when does a constraint become the work instead of enabling it?
- Composition research on heuristic prompts (Flower & Hayes, cognitive process theory) — how do prompts that describe a problem space differ from prompts that describe a product?
- Any research on prompt design for revision specifically (not first-draft generation)

**Sources to start with:**
- Patricia Goodson — *Becoming an Academic Writer* (exercises as structured constraint)
- Linda Flower & John Hayes — cognitive process model of writing
- Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi — flow and optimal challenge
- Robert Boice — *Professors as Writers* (blocking, fluency, prompt-based interventions)
- Alice Flaherty — *The Midnight Disease* (neurology of writer's block)
- Patricia Stokes — *Creativity from Constraints*
- Bonnie Friedman — *Writing Past Dark*
- Rosanne Bane — *Around the Writer's Block* (neuroscience of resistance)
- Hillary Rettig — *The 7 Secrets of the Prolific*
- Peter Elbow — *Writing Without Teachers* (free-writing as unblocking)

### 2. What block types does the current 3-way taxonomy miss?

**Why it matters:** The current taxonomy (cognitive / motivational / physiological) is clean but may be incomplete. The research should stress-test it.

**Candidate missing types:**
- **Aesthetic block.** The writer knows the structural fix but can't execute it at the quality level they want. This isn't motivational (they're not avoiding) or cognitive (they understand the problem). It's a gap between structural diagnosis and craft execution. The current system classifies this as cognitive, but the intervention (decompose, reduce variables) doesn't help — the problem isn't too many variables, it's that the writer can't produce the prose they hear in their head.
- **Decisional block.** The writer sees multiple valid structural options and can't choose. Different from cognitive overload — the problem isn't too many variables, it's genuine equipoise between alternatives. The diagnostic may have surfaced the problem but not resolved the ambiguity. Related: the "Buridan's ass" problem in revision.
- **Identity block.** The writer's self-concept is threatened by the revision. "If I cut this subplot, the book isn't the book I set out to write." "If I change this character's arc, I'm betraying my vision." This is adjacent to motivational but distinct — the writer isn't afraid of failure, they're afraid of success-that-feels-like-betrayal.
- **Accumulated-feedback block.** The writer has received too much feedback (from APODICTIC, from beta readers, from their own re-reads) and can no longer see the manuscript as a reader would. Every sentence is tagged with problems. The writer can't read their own work without the diagnostic overlay. This is an iatrogenic block — the diagnostic tool may have caused it.
- **Draft-stage mismatch.** The writer is trying to do polish-level work on a structural-level draft, or structural work on a near-final draft. The block isn't about the specific problem — it's about applying the wrong mode of attention.

**What to look for:**
- Taxonomies of writing blocks in psychology literature (is there anything more granular than Boice's categories?)
- Taxonomies from creative writing pedagogy (workshop-based classifications)
- Whether the "aesthetic block" has a name in the literature
- Whether the "accumulated-feedback block" is recognized as a phenomenon
- How other diagnostic systems (therapy, medical diagnosis, coaching) handle blocks that are iatrogenic (caused by the diagnostic process itself)

### 3. What does "proactive prompt generation" look like in practice?

**Why it matters:** This is the core build question. We need examples of structurally-informed prompts that work — prompts tied to specific diagnosed problems that help writers move without prescribing content.

**What to look for:**
- Existing prompt libraries in writing craft books — which ones are structural vs. content-prescriptive?
- How craft exercise books organize prompts by problem type (not by genre or topic)
- Whether any writing tools or courses offer diagnosis-to-prompt pipelines
- The "writing experiment" tradition (John Gardner's *The Art of Fiction* exercises, Janet Burroway's *Writing Fiction* exercises) — do any of these tie exercises to diagnosed problems?
- How music composition pedagogy uses constraint-based exercises (parallel to our problem)
- How visual art pedagogy uses constraint-based exercises
- Game design's concept of "design constraints" as creative generators

**Specific prompt patterns to investigate:**
- **Constraint prompts:** "Rewrite this scene with [structural constraint] — e.g., the character must initiate every action, no external events can drive the plot"
- **Inversion prompts:** "Write the opposite version — what happens if the character does the exact thing you're avoiding?"
- **Isolation prompts:** "Write just the emotional arc of this scene, ignoring plot. Then write just the plot, ignoring emotion. Compare."
- **Scale-shift prompts:** "Write this scene as a single paragraph. Then expand the one sentence that matters most."
- **Perspective prompts:** "Write this scene from the antagonist's POV. What do they want? What are they afraid of?"
- **Deletion prompts:** "Cut 50% of this scene. What survives? Why?"
- **Temporal prompts:** "Write the scene that happens 10 years after this one. What does the character remember?"

### 4. How should prompts map to diagnostic codes?

**Why it matters:** The module's value proposition is that prompts are structurally informed — tied to specific diagnosed problems, not generic. This means we need a mapping from diagnostic findings to prompt families.

**What to look for:**
- Can the existing root cause categories (from Diagnostic_State.md) be mapped to prompt families? E.g., "agency collapse" → constraint prompts that force character-initiated action; "pacing drag" → scale-shift prompts that compress time.
- Are there root cause categories that resist prompt generation? (If the diagnosis is "the subplot is unnecessary," the prompt is just "cut it" — there's no generative exercise.)
- How many prompt families are needed? Is it one per root cause type, or do some root causes share prompt strategies?
- Should prompts be genre-calibrated? (A horror prompt for "low tension" looks different from a romance prompt for "low tension.")
- How does this interact with the argument engine? Can nonfiction diagnosed problems map to prompts? ("Your warrant for claim 3 is missing" → "Write three different warrants for this claim without looking at your current draft.")

### 5. What's the right delivery model?

**Why it matters:** Prompts could be delivered as part of session plans, as a separate exercise library, as an interactive prompt generator, or as a standalone `/prompt` command. The delivery model affects the build.

**Options to evaluate:**
- **Embedded in session plans.** The coach already produces session plans with a focus and intervention class. Prompts could be added as "suggested exercise" when the intervention class is "structural exploration" or "unblocking."
- **Standalone exercise library.** A reference file of exercises indexed by root cause type. The coach selects from it; the writer can also browse directly.
- **Interactive prompt generator.** A `/prompt` or `/exercise` command that reads the diagnostic state and generates a tailored prompt. More dynamic but harder to quality-control.
- **Prompt sequences.** Some blocks need a sequence (warm-up → exploration → commitment), not a single prompt. How does the system handle multi-step exercises?

**Design constraints to respect:**
- The firewall. Prompts must be structural, not prescriptive.
- The diagnostic dependency. Prompts must be grounded in actual diagnostic findings, not generic.
- The coaching relationship. Prompts should feel like they come from a coach who knows the manuscript, not from a random exercise generator.
- Context cost. Each prompt/exercise that loads into context is token budget the coach can't use for other things.

### 6. What's the risk of making things worse?

**Why it matters:** The accumulated-feedback block (Q2) is a real risk. Adding a prompt generator to an already-heavy diagnostic system could deepen the problem: the writer now has a diagnosis, a session plan, AND an exercise — three layers of mediation between them and their manuscript.

**What to look for:**
- Research on over-scaffolding in writing pedagogy — when does support become interference?
- The "workshop damage" phenomenon — writers who've received so much feedback they can no longer write without it
- How therapeutic interventions manage the risk of iatrogenic harm (the treatment causing the disease)
- Whether the literature supports a "less is more" approach to writing prompts — i.e., one well-chosen prompt beats five options
- Exit criteria: how does the writer know they don't need the prompt anymore?

## What success looks like

A writer with a diagnosed root cause ("character agency collapses in Act 2 because all turning points are externally driven") gets a structurally-informed prompt ("Write the Act 2 turning point three ways, each time with the character initiating the change. Don't worry about quality — this is structural exploration. Which version makes the downstream scenes possible?") that:

1. References the specific diagnosis
2. Specifies a structural constraint (character initiates)
3. Leaves all content decisions to the writer
4. Includes a self-evaluation step (which version makes downstream work?)
5. Feels like coaching, not like an assignment

## What failure looks like

- Prompts that cross the firewall and prescribe content
- Prompts so generic they could apply to any manuscript
- Prompts that add cognitive load instead of reducing it
- A prompt library so large it requires its own navigation
- Writers who become dependent on prompts and can't revise without them
- The module generating prompts for problems that don't need them (some root causes just need cutting, not creative exploration)

## Recommended research approach

1. **Literature review** (questions 1, 2, 6) — deep research across writing pedagogy, psychology of creativity, constraint theory, and cognitive process research
2. **Prompt pattern inventory** (question 3) — survey existing craft exercise books and extract the structural-prompt patterns, ignoring the content-prescriptive ones
3. **Mapping exercise** (question 4) — take APODICTIC's existing root cause categories and attempt to map each to a prompt family; identify gaps and resistances
4. **Delivery model evaluation** (question 5) — prototype each option against 2-3 real diagnostic states and evaluate for firewall compliance, cognitive load, and coaching fit
5. **Risk assessment** (question 6) — synthesize the over-scaffolding literature into concrete design constraints

This research should produce a level-setting document (like the ones for existing audits) that grounds the build in evidence rather than intuition.

## Models to use

This research benefits from multi-model coverage:

- **Deep research mode** (Claude, Gemini, or dedicated research tools) for literature review and theoretical grounding
- **Adversarial review** (Codex or Claude in adversarial mode) to stress-test the firewall boundary and identify prompts that feel structural but are actually prescriptive
- **Pattern extraction** (any model) for the prompt inventory — reading craft exercise books and classifying prompts by type

The core risk is that a single model will converge on a prompt taxonomy that feels clean but doesn't survive contact with real diagnostic states. Cross-model pressure helps.
