Written Testimony Before the Committee on Recreation and Libraries
Public Hearing on the Fiscal Year 2027 Budget: Restoration of Weekend Hours at Branch Libraries

Good afternoon, Chairperson Alvarez and members of the Committee. My name is Dana Whitfield. I have lived in the Glenmore neighborhood for eleven years, I serve on the Friends of the Glenmore Branch Library board, and I am here to ask the Committee to restore the $1.9 million that was cut from branch weekend operations in the FY26 budget.

The cut closed nine of the city's fourteen branch libraries on Sundays and reduced Saturday service at six of them to four hours. I want to describe what that change did, using the Library Department's own numbers, and then address the two alternatives the Committee discussed at the March oversight hearing.

First, the numbers. The Department's FY26 second-quarter performance report shows systemwide weekend door counts fell 61 percent from the prior year — not because demand fell, but because the doors were shut. The five branches that kept full weekend hours saw weekend visits rise 14 percent over the same period. Demand did not disappear. It crowded into the buildings that remained open, and it left the neighborhoods that lost service with nothing. At Glenmore, Sunday circulation last year was the second-highest of any day of the week. Our Sunday patrons were not marginal users. They were the branch's core.

Second, who those patrons are. Weekend hours are not a luxury add-on for people with flexible schedules; they are the only hours available to people without them. The Department's 2025 patron survey found that 44 percent of weekend visitors reported weekends as the only time they could visit, and that weekend users were disproportionately working parents, shift workers, and students. When the Committee cuts Sunday hours, it is not trimming a service at the margin. It is selecting, with some precision, the patrons who have no substitute.

Permit me thirty seconds in the epic register, Councilmembers, because the scene deserves it: at 9:55 on a Saturday morning outside the Glenmore branch there assembles a host that Homer would recognize. Strollers drawn up in ranks like chariots. A retiree bearing his canvas sack of returns the way Ajax bore his shield. Three teenagers sworn to take the last study room or perish in the attempt. At ten the doors open, the siege resolves in ninety seconds, and by 10:04 every seat in the children's room is taken. I am exaggerating, of course — but only about the armor. The crowd is real. It is there every single week, and it is the strongest argument I know.

Third, the alternatives. At the March hearing, two were raised, and I want to take both seriously rather than wave them away.

Volunteer staffing was the first. The Friends board has organized volunteers for eleven years, and I can tell the Committee from direct experience what volunteers can and cannot do. They can run a book sale, shelve returns, and staff a story hour. They cannot open a building. Insurance, keyholding, incident response, and the collective bargaining agreement all require paid staff on site, and the Department's own counsel confirmed as much in its April memo to this Committee. Volunteers extend paid staff; they do not replace them. Any budget line that assumes otherwise is not a plan, it is a hope.

Extended weekday evenings were the second. The Department modeled this in its FY26 alternatives analysis: shifting the same staff-hours to weekday evenings recovers at most a third of lost weekend visits, because the weekend population — the working parents and shift workers I described — cannot simply move their availability to a Tuesday at 7 p.m. The evening option costs the same money to serve fewer of the people the cut harmed most. If the Committee's goal is efficiency, evenings are the less efficient choice.

I will close with the budget arithmetic. The full restoration is $1.9 million, which is roughly two-tenths of one percent of the general fund. On the Department's FY25 figures, weekend service delivered a cost per visit of $3.10, against a systemwide average of $4.75 — on that measure, the strongest per-dollar performance of any service block in the library budget. I recognize the Committee faces real tradeoffs this cycle, and I do not envy you the ledger. But this line is small, its performance is documented, and the people it serves have no substitute. Restore the weekend hours. The crowd at the door will do the rest.

Thank you, and I welcome the Committee's questions.
