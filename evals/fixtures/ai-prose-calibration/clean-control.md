Nadia's mother had kept the flat too hot, and the radiator was still ticking when Nadia let
herself in. Chipped enamel mug — the one with the hairline crack her mother had refused to
throw out — went on the windowsill, because the table was buried under three decades of
unopened bank statements. Four floors down, the number 26 bus wheezed at the stop and pulled
away without anyone getting on.

When Daniel knocked, he knocked the way he always had: two raps, a pause, one more, like he
was apologising for the first two. He didn't sit. He stood in the doorway with his coat still
buttoned, jiggling his keys, and Nadia understood he wasn't planning to stay long.

"You didn't have to come all this way," she said.

"I was going to be in the area." He was not going to be in the area. He lived forty minutes
north and hated the drive.

"The reading's Thursday," she said. "I haven't opened anything yet."

"Good. Don't." He looked at the bank statements, then at the mug on the sill, then at her.
"Whatever's in there, it'll keep till you've slept."

She wanted to argue that she had slept, that she was fine, that a person could bury a mother
and still remember to eat. Instead she picked up the mug, found the tea in it stone cold, and
drank it anyway rather than admit she'd forgotten making it an hour ago. Daniel watched her
do it and had the decency not to say anything, and that — the not saying — was the kindest
thing anyone had managed all week.
