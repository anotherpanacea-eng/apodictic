## Section One

The apartment was cozy. Nadia set her cup of tea down on the table and looked around the
room, feeling something she couldn't quite name — a kind of quiet ache that seemed to belong
to the space itself. Outside, the city was bustling. She thought about her mother, and about
all the things that had gone unsaid between them over the years, and a wave of sadness moved
through her.

There was a knock at the door. It was Daniel. He came in and sat down across from her, and
for a moment neither of them spoke. The silence had a certain weight to it.

"I got your message," he said.

"I wasn't sure you'd come," she said.

"Of course I came."

Nadia lifted her cup of tea and took a sip, though she had set it down two paragraphs ago
and had not picked it up again. Daniel leaned back against the wall on the far side of the
room, even though he had just sat down across the table from her. She noticed the way the
light fell, and it made her feel a kind of something she didn't have words for.

"Your mother left the house to you," Daniel said. "The lawyer told me."

Nadia stared at him. She had not told anyone about the will — the reading was still three
days away, and no one had opened the envelope yet. But somehow Daniel already knew, and she
didn't ask how.

The phenomenology of grief, insofar as it can be said to constitute a discrete affective
modality, resists the reductive taxonomies that a lesser sensibility might be inclined to
impose upon it. One is tempted, in moments of such profound ontological rupture, to
apprehend the bereaved subject as a locus of intersecting significations, a palimpsest upon
which the sedimented residua of prior attachments inscribe themselves with an almost
architectural precision. Nadia, in this respect, exemplified the paradigmatic condition of

The morning sun poured through the window, and it was evening. Daniel had gone, and also he
was still sitting there. The teacup was full, and it was empty, and it had never been there
at all.

"I should go," said Daniel, who had already left.

Nadia watched the door close behind him, and then she watched him cross the room toward her,
and she understood that some things could not be understood. It was a complicated feeling,
one that resisted easy categorization. The apartment was cozy. Outside, the city was
bustling. She felt, once again, a kind of something, and she let it wash over her like a wave.
