Chapter One

When Miriam Aster returned to Bellweather in October, the clocks in her mother's house had all stopped at eleven minutes past four. The solicitor called it damp. Miriam called it instruction, though she could not yet say whose.

Her mother had left the house equally to Miriam and Edmund, on the condition that they keep the west orchard until the tenant families had gathered one final crop. A railway syndicate had already offered twice the orchard's value.

Edmund wanted the sale. Miriam wanted to know why their mother, who disliked sentiment, had written the condition in her own hand beneath the will.

Chapter Two

At thirty-six, Miriam had grown used to being taken for the younger child, though her brother Edmund was two years younger. His quick confidence made him seem the elder whenever money entered a room.

"We can honor the harvest and sign in November," he said over breakfast. "No one is being dispossessed."

Miriam watched the stopped kitchen clock. Its hands matched every clock in the house. "Then waiting costs us nothing."

Chapter Three

In the escritoire she found the household accounts beginning with her marriage in 1832. Six years of ordinary entries ended with a page headed Spring, 1838. On that page their mother had paid a surveyor named Pell for an examination of the west orchard.

The next leaf had been cut out close to the binding. A dust line showed where a folded enclosure had rested for years.

Miriam remembered the spring because Edmund had come down from school with a broken wrist. He had spent April learning to write with his left hand and stealing pears before they were ripe.

Chapter Four

Pell's widow lived above the apothecary. She received Miriam beside a cage of sleeping finches and produced a brass transit level wrapped in baize.

"Your mother feared the railway cutting would drain the tenant wells," Mrs. Pell said. "My husband proved it. The company bought his silence, but he gave her a copy."

The copy, she believed, had been hidden in the Aster house. Pell had set every clock to mark the bearing from which he took his decisive measurement: four degrees and eleven minutes west.

Chapter Five

Miriam and Edmund began in the west parlor. From the center window, Pell's bearing pointed not toward the orchard but toward the old icehouse behind it.

Edmund laughed when Miriam explained the clocks, yet he brought a spade. Together they cleared nettles from the icehouse threshold and found the door newly scraped around its lock.

Inside, the brick floor showed a pale square where something heavy had recently stood. Edmund's boots carried the same white mortar dust that lay along the wall.

Chapter Six

"You found it before I arrived," Miriam said.

Edmund sat on the empty ice chest. He admitted that the syndicate's agent had paid his debts and asked for the survey in return. He had taken Pell's copy but had not yet delivered it.

From inside his coat he drew the missing enclosure. The figures showed that the proposed cutting would lower the water table enough to spoil three wells and the millstream.

"Mother knew I would sell," he said. "The condition was meant to give you time to catch me."

Chapter Seven

Miriam did not answer until they had carried the survey back to the house. Edmund, her younger by two years, waited at the table while she wrote to the tenant families and the county engineer.

She dated her account carefully: Pell's survey in the spring of 1838, six years after her marriage in 1832. The clocks had preserved not an hour but a direction, and the direction had preserved the proof.

Edmund signed the letter beside her. They refused the syndicate before dusk, and in November, when the last apples were stored, the tenant wells still ran clear beneath the west orchard.
