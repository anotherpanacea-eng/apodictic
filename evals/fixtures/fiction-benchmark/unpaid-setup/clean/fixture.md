The ferry left Elin Ward on Saint Brannoc with one suitcase and twelve hours before the auction. From the pier she could see the schoolhouse above the harbor, its windows boarded since her mother's death, and the weathercock turning against a clear sky.

Mr. Pell, the auctioneer, met her at the gate. He had tagged the desks, the stove, even the brass handbell. "Summer people like a schoolhouse," he said. "One of them will make it handsome."

Elin signed the inventory but not the sale order. She told herself the delay was practical. The island had taught her how quickly a signature could become a promise.

* * *

Her aunt Mara kept the school keys in a blue bowl by the stove. She gave Elin the iron ring, then opened her palm to show one key apart from the others: the small brass key she never let out of her sight.

"Your mother made me swear it went to you," Mara said. "Not to Pell, and not into any labeled lot."

The key was too delicate for a door. Its bow had been worn smooth on one side, as if a thumb had rubbed it for years. Elin threaded it onto the iron ring and felt its slight weight tap against her knuckle.

Mara would say nothing more. "Some promises belong to the dead until the living decide what to do with them."

* * *

At the schoolhouse, Elin worked from room to room, separating books from rubbish. Under the dust she found chalk sums in her mother's hand and a row of names penciled inside a cupboard door.

One name belonged to Tomas Bell, who appeared at noon carrying a basket of oatcakes. He was fourteen now, tall enough to look through the fanlight without climbing the step.

"Mr. Pell says they're making it cottages," he said.

"Mr. Pell says many things before they happen."

Tomas set down the basket. His younger sisters were studying by post, he told her, when the mail boat ran. He asked whether teachers in the city ever came to islands for only half a year.

Elin heard herself explain the certification rules. By the time she stopped, Tomas was smiling as if she had already agreed to something.

* * *

Rain arrived before evening and sealed them inside. Water drummed on the slate while Elin lit the stove and found a dry corner for the books.

In the back room stood her mother's desk. Every drawer opened except a narrow one above the kneehole. The iron keys were all too large, and the brass key entered halfway before catching.

Elin nearly forced it. Instead she withdrew it and noticed a tiny cabinet built into the mantel, painted the same gray as the wall. The lock was hidden behind a wooden rosette.

The gale pressed smoke down the chimney. She pocketed the key and spent the next hour clearing the flue, then walked Tomas home when the rain eased.

* * *

She returned alone after dark. The brass key turned in the mantel cabinet with a soft double click.

Inside lay her mother's tuning fork, a packet of school photographs, and a note addressed to Elin. The note did not ask her to stay. It said only that a school kept an island from becoming a place children learned to leave before they learned what it could hold.

Elin read it twice. On the back her mother had written a list of mainland teachers who had offered to share winter terms. The last name was Elin's, copied from a letter she had sent years ago and forgotten.

She put the photographs back but kept the note. When she locked the cabinet, the small key no longer felt like an obligation carried for someone else.

* * *

At nine the next morning, Mr. Pell arranged his catalogues on the front desk. Six bidders waited under umbrellas in the yard.

Elin handed him payment for his wasted journey and tore the unsigned sale order across its blank signature line. She would teach the autumn term, she said, and use the interval to recruit someone for winter.

Pell looked around at the tagged furniture. "You can't run a school on memories."

"No," Elin said. Through the window she saw Tomas climbing the hill with both sisters behind him. "But I can open one with pupils."

She rang the brass handbell and carried the auction tags to the stove. By noon the boards were off the eastern windows, and the schoolhouse faced the harbor awake.
