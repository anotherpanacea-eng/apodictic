On the morning the bridge clock stopped, Nessa Rook found a tooth of brass on the workshop floor. It had broken from the escapement, and without it the lifting bridge could not be timed safely for the mail ferry.

The council inspector was due in four days. If the clock failed his test, the ferry contract would go upriver and half the shops on Bridge Street would lose their winter trade.

Nessa removed the damaged wheel. On its hidden face she found the initials A.R., not her father's mark but those of Abel Rusk, the clockmaker he had driven out of town twenty years before.

* * *

Rusk lived in a cottage beyond the salt meadow. He opened the door only after Nessa slid the broken tooth underneath it.

"Your father filed that wheel thin," he said when she was inside. "He wanted the council to replace my mechanism with his."

Nessa did not defend the dead. She asked whether Rusk could make another wheel.

His hands were too unsteady now, but he brought out the original pattern. The alloy required bell bronze, he said; ordinary brass would warp under the bridge's load.

The foundry had no bell bronze. Rusk pointed through his window toward the ruined chapel on the marsh. Its cracked bell had not sounded in forty years.

* * *

The chapel belonged to three parishes and to none of them. Nessa spent a day finding the senior churchwarden, who refused to surrender even a broken bell for a machine built by a Rook.

She set Rusk's pattern on his desk beside the damaged wheel. "The machine was built by Rusk. My father only put his name on the case."

By evening the churchwarden had read the old council minutes and changed his answer. He gave her a written order for one handspan of bronze, provided Rusk's name was restored to the clock.

At low tide Nessa crossed the marsh with a saw and cut the metal from the bell's split lip. The fragment rang once when it fell, a small clear note over the mudflats.

* * *

The foundry master melted the bronze before dawn. Rusk stood beside the mold and directed the pour while Nessa kept the pattern level in its bed of sand.

When the new wheel cooled, its teeth were rough but true. Nessa filed each one to Rusk's gauge and carried the finished escapement to the bridge tower.

The old clock accepted it. For the first time in years, the minute hand crossed twelve without shuddering, and the bridge leaves began to rise as the mail ferry sounded from the bend.

* * *

The inspector arrived on the fourth morning. He timed three openings, tested the brake, and renewed the ferry contract before lunch.

Nessa climbed the tower afterward with a fresh brass plate. She removed the one bearing her father's name and fixed the new plate beneath the clock face: ABEL RUSK, MAKER; NESSA ROOK, RESTORER.

At noon the cracked chapel bell was silent, but its missing metal sounded in every measured stroke above Bridge Street. Rusk listened from the square until the last chime faded, then took Nessa's arm for the walk home.
