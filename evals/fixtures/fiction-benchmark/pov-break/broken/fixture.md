At dusk Jonah Vale saw the signal lamp wink twice above the flooded quarry. He stopped on the towpath, counting the interval between flashes, and decided it was meant for him.

He had come home to sell the lock house, not to answer old signals. The brass key in his pocket belonged to an estate agent now, and by noon tomorrow the last room of his childhood would belong to strangers.

Yet the lamp shone from the quarry office, a building shuttered since the pumps failed. Jonah could see no boat on the black water and no figure at the window.

He left the towpath and climbed the cinder road. Rain had softened the ruts, and each step pulled at his boots as if the hill meant to keep him below.

The office door stood open by a hand's width. Jonah called his sister's name before remembering that Clara had promised never to set foot there again.

No one answered. Inside, dust silvered the desk and the old shift ledgers leaned together on a shelf. The signal lamp sat warm beside an oil can.

Jonah touched the chimney and drew back. Someone had lit it within the hour. Beneath it lay a folded survey map with the eastern shaft circled in blue pencil.

The circle tightened his throat. Their father had died in that shaft, or so the company had said after three days of searching.

A board creaked behind him. Clara filled the doorway, rain darkening her coat. She looked older than she had at the funeral and less willing to forgive him.

"You saw it too," Jonah said.

"I saw you leave the path." Clara kept her hands in her pockets. "The lamp was already dark."

Jonah looked back. The wick smoked under the chimney, and the office held the sharp smell of extinguished oil. He could not tell whether she was contradicting him or protecting herself.

He showed her the map. Clara glanced at the circle and then at the shelf where their father's ledgers should have been.

"We should go," she said. "The ground isn't safe."

Jonah folded the map into his coat. He heard, beneath the rain on the roof, three measured knocks from somewhere under the floor.

Clara heard them too; he knew because her gaze dropped to the warped boards. Neither of them moved until the knocks came again.

The trapdoor behind the desk had swollen in its frame. Jonah found a pry bar and worked its edge loose while Clara held the lamp.

She watched him without speaking, her face composed in the way it had been whenever their father chose Jonah for an errand she wanted. She wondered whether he had ever loved her at all.

The trap opened on a ladder and a wet breath of stone. Jonah remembered the company men sealing this passage, but he also remembered how easily company men lied.

"Stay here," he said.

Clara gave him the lamp. "That worked so well last time."

He descended. The rungs trembled under his weight, and at the bottom a narrow drainage gallery ran toward the eastern shaft.

Fresh boot marks stippled the mud. Jonah crouched over them, measuring the heel against his palm, and heard Clara start down after him.

Clara followed without waiting for him to ask, her boots ringing on the iron rungs. She wished he would ask her to come instead of ordering her to remain behind.

They followed the marks to a timber door. Someone had cut the company's seal and looped a new chain through the handles without locking it.

Beyond the door, their lamp found a dry chamber, a camp stool, and a tin dispatch box. Jonah recognized the box from his father's desk.

His hands shook as he raised the lid. The first pages were pay sheets; beneath them lay letters documenting cracks in the eastern retaining wall weeks before the collapse.

"He knew," Clara said.

Jonah read the foreman's signature twice. Their father had reported the danger. The company had ordered the shift to continue and then blamed him for entering a closed passage alone.

A scrape sounded beyond the chamber. Jonah shuttered the lamp and listened, but whoever had made the boot marks did not approach.

The letter Jonah had mailed before leaving the city had not yet reached the buyer; he could only hope the sale could still be stopped. Across town, the letter still lay unopened on the hall table.

He carried the dispatch box back to the ladder. By the time they reached the office, the rain had thinned and a pale strip of morning showed over the quarry.

Clara set the signal lamp in the window. Jonah understood then that she had lit it, not to summon him into danger, but to make certain he could not leave without seeing what their father had tried to save.

"You might have told me," he said.

"You might have stayed long enough to listen."

Jonah took the estate agent's key from his pocket and laid it beside the lamp. They walked down the hill together to telephone the inspector, leaving the lock house unsold and the old signal burning behind them.
