# Policy Brief: Make Metro Transit Fare-Free in Rivermont

**Prepared for the Rivermont City Council — Transportation Committee**

## Summary

Rivermont should eliminate all fares on the Metro Transit bus and light-rail
system. Fare-free transit will raise ridership, cut transportation
emissions, and remove a regressive cost that falls hardest on low-income
residents. The council should adopt a fare-free policy effective next fiscal
year.

## The problem

Metro ridership has fallen 19 percent since 2019. Meanwhile, transportation
is now Rivermont's single largest source of greenhouse-gas emissions, at 38
percent of the city total. Low-income residents spend a disproportionate
share of household income on transportation: in the lowest income quintile,
the figure reaches 16 percent, against 4 percent in the highest.

## The case for fare-free transit

The benefits are well documented in cities that have moved in this
direction:

- **Ridership.** Peer systems that reduced or removed fares have reported
  ridership gains in the range of 20 to 60 percent within two years. Lower
  cost lowers the barrier to riding.
- **Emissions.** Every trip shifted from a private car to transit reduces
  per-capita emissions. A ridership gain of even 30 percent would move
  Rivermont meaningfully toward its 2030 climate target.
- **Equity.** Fares are a flat charge that consumes a larger share of a
  low-income rider's budget. Removing them is a direct, progressive transfer
  to the residents who most depend on the system.
- **Boarding speed.** Eliminating fare collection speeds boarding and
  improves on-time performance, which itself attracts riders.

Each of these effects is real and each points the same direction. Taken
together, they make the case decisive.

## Recommendation

The council should make Metro Transit fare-free beginning in the next fiscal
year. Fare-free transit is cleaner, fairer, and better for the city. The
evidence on its benefits is clear, and the residents of Rivermont deserve a
transit system built around access rather than collection. We urge the
committee to advance the policy without delay.
