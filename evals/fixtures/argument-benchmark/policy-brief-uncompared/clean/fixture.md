# Policy Brief: Make Metro Transit Fare-Free in Rivermont

**Prepared for the Rivermont City Council — Transportation Committee**

## Summary

Rivermont should eliminate all fares on the Metro Transit bus and light-rail
system. Fare-free transit will raise ridership, cut transportation
emissions, and remove a regressive cost that falls hardest on low-income
residents. The council should adopt a fare-free policy effective next fiscal
year.

## The problem

Metro ridership has fallen 19 percent since 2019. Meanwhile, transportation
is now Rivermont's single largest source of greenhouse-gas emissions, at 38
percent of the city total. Low-income residents spend a disproportionate
share of household income on transportation: in the lowest income quintile,
the figure reaches 16 percent, against 4 percent in the highest.

## The case for fare-free transit

The benefits are well documented in cities that have moved in this
direction:

- **Ridership.** Peer systems that reduced or removed fares have reported
  ridership gains in the range of 20 to 60 percent within two years. Lower
  cost lowers the barrier to riding.
- **Emissions.** Every trip shifted from a private car to transit reduces
  per-capita emissions. A ridership gain of even 30 percent would move
  Rivermont meaningfully toward its 2030 climate target.
- **Equity.** Fares are a flat charge that consumes a larger share of a
  low-income rider's budget. Removing them is a direct, progressive transfer
  to the residents who most depend on the system.
- **Boarding speed.** Eliminating fare collection speeds boarding and
  improves on-time performance, which itself attracts riders.

Each of these effects is real and each points the same direction. Taken
together, they make the case decisive.

## Weighing the alternative: service investment

The strongest objection to fare-free transit is not that its benefits are
unreal but that the same money might buy more of them a different way. The
leading same-goal alternative is service investment — raising frequency and
extending span so buses come every 10 minutes instead of every 20. The two
options weigh out differently on the criteria that matter to this committee:

- **Ridership per dollar.** The honest reading favors service. Frequency
  studies report ridership elasticities that generally beat fare elimination
  per dollar spent, and several of the "20 to 60 percent" fare-free results
  above were reached only where service was already frequent. On this
  criterion, service wins.
- **Emissions per dollar.** Roughly a wash, since the emissions benefit
  tracks the ridership gained.
- **Equity incidence.** Here fare-free wins and service does not substitute:
  only fare elimination removes the regressive per-trip charge itself, and it
  reaches lowest-quintile riders whether or not new service runs near them,
  whereas frequency gains concentrate on already-dense corridors that skew
  higher-income.

The tradeoff, stated plainly: service investment likely buys more total
ridership per dollar, while fare-free is the only lever that discharges the
regressive-cost problem the council named as its first reason to act. Because
that equity harm is the decisive consideration here, and because the two are
not mutually exclusive, the recommendation is fare-free as the core policy,
paired with the phased frequency investment the funding plan below sizes —
not fare-free instead of service.

## Recommendation

The council should make Metro Transit fare-free beginning in the next fiscal
year. Fare-free transit is cleaner, fairer, and better for the city. The
evidence on its benefits is clear, and the residents of Rivermont deserve a
transit system built around access rather than collection. We urge the
committee to advance the policy without delay.

## Cost and funding

Fare-free is not costless, and a brief to a budget committee has to say what
it costs and who pays. Metro collected 14.2 million dollars in fares last
year at a farebox-recovery ratio near 18 percent; eliminating fares forgoes
that revenue but returns roughly 1.1 million in collection and enforcement
costs, for a net operating gap of about 13.1 million dollars a year. Two
thirds of the gap is covered by dedicating the surplus from the downtown
curbside-parking and congestion-permit program, which ran a 9.2 million
dollar surplus last year and is projected to grow as parking prices rise; the
remaining 4 million is the first claim on the same dedicated transit fund that
finances the phased frequency investment above. The sequence is deliberate:
fare elimination begins next fiscal year, the frequency build-out follows over
the two years after, and if the parking surplus falls short in any year fares
are restored on a published trigger rather than service being cut — the fund's
shortfall rule protects frequency first.
