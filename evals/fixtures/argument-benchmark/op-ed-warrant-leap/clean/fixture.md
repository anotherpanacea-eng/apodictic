# Ban the Scooters Before Someone Else Gets Hurt

When the rental e-scooters arrived in Westhaven eighteen months ago, the
city council called them "a clean, cheap answer to our downtown traffic."
The council should now answer for what came next.

I asked Westhaven General for its emergency-department numbers, and the
hospital's own data is not in dispute. In the year before the scooters
launched, the ED logged 41 visits coded as "wheeled-transport injury." In
the year after, it logged 132 — a 222 percent increase. Three of those
patients were admitted with head trauma. The hospital's trauma director
confirmed the figures in writing and noted that the spike "tracks the
period since the scooter program began." These are not estimates or
projections. They are counted visits, drawn from the same coding system the
hospital has used for a decade.

The pattern is just as clear in the 911 logs, which I obtained through a
records request. Calls mentioning "scooter" went from zero to 58 in the same
window. The fire department's own quarterly report flags downtown scooter
incidents as a "growing call category."

Counts alone cannot carry the causal claim, so I put the numbers to two
further tests: how many rides produced the injuries, and what else changed
downtown in the same window. Under the city's data-sharing rule, the scooter
operator reported roughly 210,000 rides in that first year — about 63
injuries per 100,000 rides. The modes those rides displaced are not close:
transportation-safety tallies put private bicycles near 20 and short car
trips near 4 per 100,000 over comparable distances, so a scooter trip is on
the order of three times as likely to end in the emergency department as the
bicycle trip it replaced, and the three head-trauma admissions have no
counterpart in the prior year. Two confounders could still explain the rise
away. If scooters had merely pulled riders off more dangerous car trips, the
citywide injury total would be flat — but the trauma registry shows no
offsetting drop in car-occupant injuries. If more people were simply outdoors
and active, non-wheeled injuries would have climbed too — they did not move.
The denominator and the confounders both survive the test.

So the evidence is settled: emergency injuries rose sharply, and they rose
exactly when the scooters appeared.

A ban is a heavy remedy, and it earns its place only if the lighter measures
have been shown to fail. They have. The operator's app has recommended
helmets since launch, yet the city's own spot counts put downtown helmet use
in the low single digits, and a dockless rental gives the city no point of
enforcement — all three head-trauma riders were unhelmeted. The engineering
fix fared no better: a 15-mile-per-hour geofenced speed cap, imposed in the
program's eighth month, produced no measurable drop in the injury rate over
the two quarters that followed, and geofencing the three worst blocks only
pushed crashes onto the streets beside them. Helmet rules, speed caps, and
geofencing have each been tried on this system and each has failed — which is
the condition under which ending the program is the proportionate step and
not an overreaction.

The city must therefore ban rental e-scooters from Westhaven streets. The
council promised us a safer, cleaner downtown. Instead, our emergency room
is absorbing the cost of a convenience nobody voted for. Every month the
program continues is another month of preventable trauma. The numbers have
spoken. It is time for the council to listen and shut the program down.
