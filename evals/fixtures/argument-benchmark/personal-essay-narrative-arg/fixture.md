# The Casseroles Stop

The casseroles stop after three weeks. I learned this the way you learn the
depth of a stair in the dark — by the absence where I expected something to
be. Week one, the refrigerator would not close. Week two, I was rationing
foil pans. Week four, I stood in a clean kitchen at six in the evening and
could not remember how dinner used to happen.

A grief pamphlet from the hospital said the "acute phase" lasts six to eight
weeks. It had a chart. The line went down and to the right, a tidy decline,
as if sorrow were a fever that breaks.

In month five I reorganized the bookshelf and found his handwriting in the
margin of a paperback — *ha, no* — and sat on the floor for an hour. The
chart did not have a line for the bookshelf.

My manager was kind. He said to take the time I needed, and he meant it, and
he also asked, gently, in week six, whether I was "coming back to myself." I
said yes because it was the sentence that ended the conversation. The chart
agreed with him.

There is a casserole logic to the way we are taught to mourn. It assumes the
need is largest at the start and shrinks on a schedule. It is built for the
people bringing the food, who can sustain three weeks of attention and then
must return to their own refrigerators. I do not blame them. I was one of
them, last year, for someone else. I brought a lasagna in week one and
nothing in month five, and I told myself the quiet meant she was healing.

What I know now is that the casseroles stop on a schedule and the grief does
not. They are two different clocks, and we have agreed, as a kindness to the
living who must keep living, to pretend they are the same clock.

I keep one foil pan in the cupboard. I do not use it. It is the most honest
object in my kitchen.
