## Chapter 1 — The Wickham Light, October 1897

Elias Vane had kept the Wickham Light for eleven years, and in all that time the lamp had
never once gone dark. He was proud of that. He wrote it in the log every morning in his small
careful hand, the same hand his father had taught him before the cholera took him in '71.

The storm came up from the southwest a little after four. Elias climbed the hundred and
thirty steps to the lantern room, trimmed the wick, and set the great lens turning. Below him
the sea threw itself against the rocks the way it always did, and he felt the old familiar
calm settle over him. He had never been afraid of the sea. The sea was honest.

At half past five he saw the ship.

She was a three-master, running before the wind with her topsails gone, and she was headed
straight for the Dagger Reef. Elias knew every plank of that reef. He had pulled eleven men
off it in his years at the light, and buried four more in the churchyard above the town.

He did the only thing he could do. He lit the warning flare and he prayed.

## Chapter 2 — The Inquiry, November 1897

"You're telling me," said the man from the Board, "that you saw the vessel at half past five,
and that you took no action for a full hour."

"That's not what I said."

"It's what the log says." The man turned the book around so Elias could see his own careful
handwriting. "Half past five: sighted three-master, distressed. Half past six: lit flare."

Elias stared at the page. He did not remember writing the second line. He remembered lighting
the flare the moment he saw her — he was certain of it — but the log was in his hand, and the
log did not lie. That was the whole point of a log.

"The Marigold went onto the Dagger with all hands," the man said. "Nineteen souls. If the
flare had been lit an hour sooner, the pilot boat might have reached her."

"I lit it when I saw her." But even as he said it, Elias felt the certainty going soft, the
way a memory does when you press on it. Perhaps he had waited. Perhaps he had stood at the
glass and watched, the way he had once, long ago, watched a thing he had never told anyone
about.

The man from the Board leaned forward. He had already decided, Elias could see that. Men from
Boards always decided first and inquired after.

"The lamp never went dark," Elias said. "In eleven years. You can write that down."

"No one is questioning the lamp, Mr. Vane."

But someone was. Because the next morning, when Elias climbed the hundred and thirty steps,
the great lens had been turning all night on its own, and the log on the desk was open to a
page he had not written, in a hand that was not his, and it read: *You watched. You always
watched.*
