The forklift had been dead since Tuesday, which meant the pallets of tinned tomatoes were
still stacked three high in the loading bay where they had no business being, blocking the
route the delivery van needed to back into by six.

"Battery or starter?" Reuben asked, wiping his hands on a rag that made them dirtier.

"If I knew that," said Fen, "I'd have fixed it Tuesday." She had the panel open and was
following a wire with her thumb the way her father had taught her, feeling for the break
rather than looking for it. "Pass me the torch. Not that one. The one with tape on it."

Reuben passed her the one with tape on it. Somewhere out in the yard a reversing lorry beeped
and beeped and then stopped.

"We could hand-ball the tomatoes," he said, without enthusiasm.

"Forty crates. By six." Fen didn't look up. "You hand-ball forty crates and I'll write the
incident report when your back goes."

The break was where she'd thought it would be, a corroded spade connector behind the seat
mount, green with the kind of damp that gets into everything near the coast. She scraped it
back to bright metal with her pocketknife, pushed the connector home, and told Reuben to try
it. The forklift coughed, caught, and settled into its rattling idle.

"You're a genius," Reuben said.

"I'm cheaper than a genius," Fen said, and started shifting the first pallet before the van
arrived.
