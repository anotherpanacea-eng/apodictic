The back room of the Rosewater smelled of bruised limes and somebody's clove cigarette, and
Priya found Devi where she always found her on a Thursday — wedged into the corner booth with
the crossword, pretending she hadn't been watching the door.

"You came," Devi said. She didn't look up. She never looked up first; it was a whole thing,
a small ongoing negotiation of who would admit to wanting whom, and after four months Priya
had learned to let her win it.

Priya slid in beside her, close, so that her thigh pressed the length of her thigh. She took
the pen out of her hand and set it on the table. She turned her face toward her and waited
until she finally looked back, and when she did, her eyes did that thing, that softening at
the edges, and Priya forgot for a second which of them she was and which of them she wasn't.

"You should know," Devi said carefully, "that being bisexual isn't a phase for me, and it
isn't me being greedy, and it doesn't mean I'll leave you for a man. A lot of people assume
those things. I just wanted to explain that to you, so you understand what bisexuality
actually is."

Priya, who had marched under the same bi pride banner as Devi two summers running, who had
in fact been standing next to her when the banner tore in the wind on Canal Street, said
nothing. She reached up and tucked a strand of hair behind her ear instead, and let her hand
stay against the side of her neck, where she could feel the pulse going.

Around them the Rosewater did what it did on a Thursday: Marco behind the bar arguing about
the darts league, old Sylvie holding court by the jukebox, the whole warm familiar racket of
a place that had held them all for twenty years. Devi leaned into the hand at her throat.

"Come home with me," Priya said.

"I can't stay long. I have the early shift." But she was already gathering her coat.

They didn't make it as far as the bedroom. In the narrow hall, with the door barely shut,
Priya pressed her back against the wall and kissed her the way she'd been thinking about
kissing her since Tuesday, and she made a sound into her mouth that Priya felt in her own
chest.

Three weeks later Devi was dead — a bus, a wet road, nothing anyone could have done — and
Priya kept the crossword pen in her coat pocket for a year, the way you keep the small
ordinary things when the large impossible one has been taken away.
