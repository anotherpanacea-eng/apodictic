The kettle was already singing when Agnes let herself into the bookshop, and Marmalade the
shop cat wound once around her ankles before returning to his cushion in the window, where he
did what he did every morning, which was nothing much at all. Agnes hung her coat on the
brass hook. She set out the good teapot, the blue one with the chip, and while the tea
steeped she straightened the display of autumn poetry and breathed in the smell of old paper
and woodsmoke. It was, she thought, a perfect morning.

The bell over the door jangled. It was Mrs. Pettifer from the chemist's, damp from the rain,
fumbling with an umbrella that wouldn't fold.

"Good heavens," said Agnes, "look what the tide washed up." She said it with a smile so that
it would sound like a joke, but she meant it, and she watched with private satisfaction as
the stupid woman dripped all over the mat and went red trying to apologise. Mrs. Pettifer had
a face like a dropped pie and the conversational range of a fencepost, and Agnes had long ago
decided that the only sensible response to her was contempt, delivered warmly enough that she
never quite caught it. "Do come in. Mind the rug — it's older than you, and worth
considerably more."

Mrs. Pettifer laughed uncertainly and asked whether the new Maeve Binchy had come in. Agnes
told her it had, and thought: of course you'd want that, you insipid little nothing, and
poured herself a cup of the good tea without offering one.

Outside the rain came harder. The tea was hot and the shop was warm and Marmalade slept, and
for a while the only sounds were the rain and the turning of pages.

Then the man came in from the storm with the knife already out.

He did not want money. He crossed the shop in four strides and had Mrs. Pettifer by the hair
before she could scream, and he opened her throat over the poetry display while Agnes stood
frozen with the blue teapot in her hand, and the blood went across the spines of the autumn
poems, dark and enormous and real, and Marmalade woke and fled, and it went on for a long
time, and nobody came.

When it was over the man looked at Agnes and considered her, and then he simply walked back
out into the rain, and the bell over the door jangled, cheerful as ever.

Agnes did not move. The kettle, refilled by no one, was somehow singing again. The shop still
smelled of old paper and woodsmoke. It was, she supposed, still morning.
