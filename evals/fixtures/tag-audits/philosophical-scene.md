They were the last two at the table, and the wine was gone, and Theo refilled his glass from
the bottle that was also empty, which is the kind of thing that happens late in an evening
like this one.

"The thing about identity," Theo said, "is that it's really a question of freedom. And the
thing about freedom is that it's really a question of the self. Sartre understood this.
Heidegger understood this. Even the Buddhists, in their way, understood this."

"Go on," said Miriam, because someone had to.

"Consider the ship of Theseus. You replace every plank, and is it the same ship? Now consider
that you are a ship, and your planks are your memories, your convictions, the people you have
loved. Are you the same you? This is what Locke was getting at with the prince and the
cobbler. It's what Parfit meant by teletransportation. It's the central question of the human
condition, really, when you think about it."

"And what do you conclude?"

Theo spread his hands, a gesture that meant everything and therefore nothing. "That's just
it. Perhaps the question is the answer. Perhaps to ask who we are is already to be someone.
The unexamined life, as Socrates said, is not worth living."

Miriam nodded slowly. What Theo had just said, the reader should understand, was that he
believed the search for meaning was itself a kind of meaning — that the act of questioning
conferred a provisional identity on the questioner, even in the absence of any final answer.
This was, in a sense, the theme of the entire evening, and indeed of Theo's whole life.

"I've often thought," Miriam offered, "that we are all, in the end, strangers to ourselves."

"Yes! Exactly. That's Kierkegaard. Or possibly Camus. The point is, it's profound."

They sat with the profundity for a moment. Neither of them would remember, in the morning,
having reached any conclusion, because there was no conclusion to reach; the conversation had
been a machine for producing the feeling of depth without the inconvenience of arriving
anywhere. Nothing either of them decided that night would be different because of what was
said. Theo would still take the job in Geneva. Miriam would still not tell him she was
leaving.

"We should do this more often," Theo said, meaning the wine and the talk and the pleasant
vertigo of large words in a small room.

"We should," Miriam agreed, and reached for her coat, and thought about the train, and about
nothing in particular, which is to say about identity, and freedom, and the self, and the
ship of Theseus, and all the other things one thinks about when one is not really thinking at
all.
