Claim Ladder:
  C0: Our social rituals around grief – exemplified by casseroles delivered immediately after a death – assume that need is greatest at the beginning and diminishes on schedule, but grief persists long after the casseroles stop; the clocks of support and sorrow are misaligned.
  C1: In the author’s experience, casseroles and other tokens of support arrive for roughly three weeks and then cease, leaving a clean kitchen and an unexpected absence.
  C2: Grief does not subside on the same timetable; months later, ordinary activities (reorganizing a bookshelf and finding a loved one’s handwriting) can trigger acute sorrow.
  C3: The schedule of care reflects the needs and capacities of the living, who can sustain only a few weeks of focused attention; collectively we pretend this schedule matches grief as a kindness to those who must return to their own lives.
  Stakes: Moral and emotional – misunderstanding the duration of grief can isolate the bereaved and mask the inadequacy of communal support; recognizing the mismatch invites us to reconsider how we show up for people in mourning.
  Key terms: “casserole logic” = the assumption that grief declines on a predictable timetable mirrored by a diminishing supply of casseroles; “acute phase” = a clinical concept of grief lasting six to eight weeks.
  Codes: No major CL codes fired – the main claim is clear and subclaims follow logically. However, the normative conclusion remains implicit and derives entirely from personal testimony, leaving a subclaim gap if interpreted as universal (CL2) when measured against a broader audience.

Burden / Objections:
  Claim scope: The conclusion is implicitly universal (“we are taught to mourn” and “they are two different clocks”), implying that all or most social rituals follow the casserole logic. Evidence scope: One person’s experience and introspection. Match: No – extrapolating from a single case to a general social critique constitutes scope creep. Code: BP2.
  Comparative burden: The author does not consider whether some people prefer brief gestures or whether extended support might be intrusive. Alternatives (e.g., structured long‑term support, bereavement leave policies) are not discussed. Code: BP5 if one interprets the piece as prescribing changes, though the piece is more reflective than prescriptive.
  Precision justified: The grief pamphlet’s chart is critiqued without exploring its clinical basis; the author does not test whether six to eight weeks matches typical trajectories. Because the claim is qualitative, precision codes are less relevant.
  Strongest objection: Some might argue that grief rituals are diverse and personal; casseroles may stop but other forms of support (calls, texts, memorials) continue; the community must balance empathy with its own obligations. Engaged: No – the piece does not address alternative perspectives or variations in mourning practices. Severity: Minor, given the testimonial genre. Code: OB0 (no objections engaged) with the caveat that AT4 pieces need not handle objections.
  Dialectical integrity: The essay makes no pretence of universality; it is clearly personal, though it occasionally slips into “we” and “they” language. No manipulative moves are evident. Codes: NE1 (vignette as substitute) and NE3 (emotional override) capture the reliance on narrative rather than dialectical engagement.

Priority diagnosis:
  Structural break: The essay’s main vulnerability is that it asks a single personal story to bear a universal claim about how societies mourn. The argument is implicit and arises through narrative rather than explicit claim and support. This Anecdote‑to‑Principle Leap (FM‑A17) does not make the piece unsound because the genre invites interpretation, but it does mean that readers cannot evaluate the claim against broader evidence. The Persuasion Machine pattern (FM‑A3) is also present: vivid scenes generate conviction without a clear logical chain.
  Why it matters: Readers may feel seen in the author’s experience but cannot assess whether the mismatch between casseroles and grief is widespread or systemic. Without broader support or engagement with other experiences, the piece risks turning personal pain into a generalized critique that may not hold for all. Recognizing this helps editors decide whether to classify the piece as personal essay (testimonial) or as an argument needing more support.
  First repair target: If the author intends to make a general argument about mourning practices, they should signal the limits of personal testimony, supply broader evidence (e.g., research on grief support), and consider alternative perspectives. If the goal is purely expressive, reframe the generalizations to avoid implying universality.
