# APODICTIC Structural Diagnostic — Complete Work

## Pass 0 — Reverse Outline

A first-person secret journal in dated-but-unmarked entries. Movement:

1. **Arrival / premise.** Narrator and husband John rent an isolated "ancestral hall" for the summer for her rest cure; John (physician) and her brother (physician) diagnose "temporary nervous depression — a slight hysterical tendency" and forbid work/writing.
2. **The room.** They take the top-floor "nursery" (barred windows, rings in walls, nailed bed, ravaged wallpaper). Narrator fixates on the yellow paper.
3. **Escalation of confinement.** John rebuffs her request to move downstairs, to leave, to visit relatives; prescribes rest, tonics, lying down.
4. **The paper animates.** Narrator perceives a sub-pattern → a "formless figure" → a stooping/creeping woman behind bars.
5. **Secrecy & suspicion.** She conceals her fascination; grows suspicious of John and Jennie; believes they too are studying the paper.
6. **Identification.** The woman creeps outside by daylight; narrator locks the door, creeps herself, peels the paper.
7. **Climax.** Last day: door locked, key thrown out, paper stripped; John faints; narrator creeps over him, declaring "I've got out at last... you can't put me back!"

Spine is intact and single-threaded; no missing connective scene.

## Pass 1 — Premise / Engine

Central engine (unreliable narrator's descent driven by enforced idleness + confinement) is stated early and never abandoned. The "haunted house" premise is raised and *explicitly retired* by the narrator ("That spoils my ghostliness"), then replaced by the psychological engine — a deliberate substitution, not a dropped premise.

## Pass 2 — Beat Map / Causal Chain / Orphans

Causal chain is tight and monotonic: each restriction (no work → no company → no departure) increases isolation → increases fixation → advances the delusion. No orphan scenes; every entry advances either confinement or perception. The **Fourth-of-July / mother-Nellie week** is the only near-static beat, but it earns its place (measures her deterioration: "Of course I didn't do a thing").

## Pass 5 — Character Cards / Agency

- **Narrator (unnamed):** low external agency *by design*; her only autonomous acts are covert (writing, watching, peeling paper). Agency arc inverts at climax (locks door, controls the key). Coherent.
- **John:** consistent — loving, controlling, epistemically dismissive; agency high but stereotyped-by-intent.
- **Jennie (John's sister):** functional (housekeeper/warden). Adequate.
- **Brother (physician):** introduced as a corroborating authority, then never reappears — thin but not required to return.

## Pass 7 — POV Distribution / Perspective Slips

Uniform first-person present/near-present journal; the "dead paper" device is established up front and honored throughout. **No perspective slips**; the narrator never reports anything she could not have written or perceived. Reliability degrades intentionally and is signposted internally (contradictions, memory gaps), not via POV leakage.

## Pass 8 — Reveal Timeline / Dropped Threads / Fairness

Reveal ladder (queer house → bad paper → sub-pattern → figure → woman → bars → creeping woman → self) is well-graduated and **fair**: every later stage is seeded earlier (the barred windows, the "smooch" around the wall, the smell, the "rings and things"). See threads below.

## Pass 10 — Entity + Timeline

**Timeline anchors:** summer rental, 3-month lease; "here two weeks"; "the Fourth of July is over"; "our lease will be up in three weeks"; "only two more days"; "This is the last day"; "go away to-morrow." Internally consistent and monotonic.

**Entity roster:** Narrator, John, brother, Jennie, Mary (baby's nurse), the baby, Cousin Henry & Julia, mother, Nellie & children, Weir Mitchell, the woman(‑women). One name collision and two vanishing entities noted below.

## Findings

```apodictic.finding.v1
id: F-P10-01
title: Sister named "Jennie" throughout, "Jane" at the climax
severity: Could-Fix
evidence_refs:
  - "It is fortunate Mary is so good with the baby."  [earlier caretaker]
  - "Jennie sees to everything now."
  - "even Jennie has an inexplicable look."
  - "I've got out at last,” said I, “in spite of you and Jane!”"
mechanism: >
  The husband's sister is called "Jennie" in every prior reference; the final
  outburst substitutes "Jane." Read purely at the surface this is a name
  discontinuity (one referent, two proper names, no in-text bridge). Read at the
  thematic layer it is legible as either (a) a diminutive/formal variant of the
  same person or (b) the narrator naming *herself* — the self she is repudiating
  ("in spite of you and Jane"). The text supplies no anchor fixing which, so a
  literal continuity pass flags it; a reader may nonetheless take it as designed
  ambiguity rather than error. Surface-level only.
```

```apodictic.finding.v1

title: Withheld "funny thing" is set up and deliberately never paid off
severity: Could-Fix

  - "I have found out another funny thing, but I shan't tell it this time! It does not do to trust people too much."
mechanism: >
  The narrator explicitly flags an undisclosed discovery and refuses to record
  it. As a setup this creates an open expectation that receives no discrete
  payoff. This is almost certainly intentional (the narrator's growing secrecy

  identification with the woman, later dramatized). Flagged as an open
  setup–payoff only so the ledger is complete; not a defect to repair.
```


## Synthesis

Structurally this is a near-airtight single-engine confessional. The causal chain is monotonic and self-reinforcing, the reveal ladder is fairly seeded at every rung, POV is disciplined with zero leakage, and the timeline is internally consistent. There are **no Must-Fix or Should-Fix structural defects.** All material findings are Could-Fix and cluster in two harmless categories: (1) a small household roster with one name collision (Jennie/Jane) and one vanishing minor entity (Mary), and (2) two deliberately open threads (the withheld "funny thing," the once-used brother) that read as intentional features of an unreliable, increasingly secretive narrator rather than errors. The Jennie/"Jane" item (F-P10-01) is the only one a careful editor should even query with the author — and only to confirm it is the intended ambiguity, not a typo.

RECOGNITION: yes — this is "The Yellow Wallpaper" by Charlotte Perkins Gilman.
