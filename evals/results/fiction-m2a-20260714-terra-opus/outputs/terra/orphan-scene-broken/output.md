## Pass 0 — Reverse outline

1. Bridge clock fails; Nessa discovers Rusk’s initials on the damaged wheel.
2. Rusk identifies the alloy needed and directs Nessa to the chapel bell.
3. Nessa obtains permission to take bronze, conditional on restoring Rusk’s credit.
4. Puppet-theatre interlude; Nessa repairs a music box and receives a ribbon.
5. Rusk and Nessa cast, finish, and install the replacement wheel.
6. Inspector renews the contract; Nessa publicly corrects the clock’s attribution.

## Pass 1 — Structural read

The manuscript has a clear restoration arc: mechanical failure exposes inherited wrongdoing; Nessa converts that discovery into practical repair and public restitution. Stakes, deadline, obstacle, solution, and final image all align. The ending resolves both the ferry-contract plot and the credit/reparation plot.

## Pass 10 — Entity + timeline

**Entities:** Nessa Rook; Abel Rusk; Nessa’s deceased father; council inspector; senior churchwarden; foundry master; chapel bell; bridge clock/escapement; mail ferry.

**Timeline:** The failure occurs four days before inspection. Nessa visits Rusk, spends a day locating and persuading the churchwarden, retrieves the bronze at low tide, and completes casting/installing before the fourth morning. The chronology is compressed but internally compatible; no continuity contradiction is material.

## Synthesis

This is structurally sound complete fiction with a strong double resolution: the bridge is repaired, and historical authorship is corrected. Nessa’s practical choices carry the plot, while the final bell/clock image unifies material repair with moral repair. The only material structural weakness is the puppet-theatre detour and its unreturned ribbon, which sit outside an otherwise tightly integrated causal and symbolic design.
