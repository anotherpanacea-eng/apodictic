## Pass 0 — Reverse outline

1. Narrator and John rent an isolated ancestral estate for summer.
2. Her physicians dismiss her illness; John forbids work and directs rest.
3. John selects the barred nursery despite her preference for a downstairs room.
4. Secret writing establishes the narrator’s private counter-voice.
5. The wallpaper becomes her principal object of attention and irritation.
6. Isolation, infant separation, and domestic dependency intensify her distress.
7. John refuses to alter the room or permit stimulating company.
8. The wallpaper’s apparent sub-pattern resolves into a woman behind it.
9. The narrator seeks removal; John defers it until the lease ends and invalidates her judgment.
10. Day/night pattern changes produce a prison-bars interpretation.
11. She begins concealing wakefulness and suspecting John and Jennie of studying the paper.
12. Her goal reverses from leaving the house to remaining long enough to “find it out.”
13. She identifies herself with the creeping woman and plans to remove the paper.
14. On the last day, she isolates herself, strips the wallpaper, locks the door, and readies a rope.
15. John enters, sees her transformed behavior, faints, and she continues creeping over him.

## Pass 1 — Macro structure

The manuscript has a complete, escalating containment structure: medical dismissal → spatial confinement → secret observation → private explanatory system → covert action → irreversible public confrontation. The house and wallpaper are not detached atmosphere; each becomes a mechanism that narrows the narrator’s available choices.

No Must-Fix macro-structural gap is evident.

## Pass 2 — Beat map, causal chain, orphan scenes

The causal chain is continuous:

`dismissal of illness → imposed rest/room → loss of work and companionship → fixation on wallpaper → failed appeal to leave → concealment → identification with the trapped woman → paper removal → locked-room confrontation`

No scene is materially orphaned. The garden, barred windows, nailed bed, torn paper, daytime sleeping, Jennie’s observations, and the lease deadline each later support either confinement, surveillance, deterioration, or the climax.

The narrator’s decisive actions—writing secretly, monitoring the paper, declining to leave, locking the door, stripping the paper—are all motivated and progressively escalated. Her agency is structurally strong even as its outcome is catastrophic.

## Pass 7 — POV distribution and slips

The story maintains a single first-person journal perspective. There are no perspective slips into knowledge the narrator could not possess. The increasing instability is conveyed through interpretation rather than an accidental breach of viewpoint.

## Pass 8 — Reveal timeline, threads, fairness

The woman-behind-the-paper reveal is fairly seeded and progressively clarified:

- “a strange… formless sort of figure”
- “a woman stooping down and creeping”
- the paper becomes “bars”
- “I wonder if they all come out of that wallpaper as I did?”

The ending’s “Jane” is the one materially ambiguous reveal cue. It can function as self-identification, but the manuscript has not established a clear prior referent for “Jane”; John’s sister has been called “Jennie.”

## Pass 10 — Entity and timeline

- The manuscript establishes a summer rental and a three-month stay.
- It explicitly marks arrival, two weeks in residence, post–Fourth of July, three weeks remaining, one week remaining, two days remaining, and the final day.
- The chronology supports the deterioration; no material time contradiction is apparent.
- Mary and Jennie remain distinct: Mary cares for the baby; Jennie is John’s sister and household manager.
- “Jane” remains the only unresolved entity reference.

## Synthesis

This is a tightly integrated complete manuscript, not a fragment. Its structural strength is the conversion of care into confinement and then confinement into the narrator’s self-authorized escape ritual. The ending is prepared by concrete physical details rather than arriving as a merely symbolic rupture.

The sole material editorial question is whether the final “Jane” ambiguity is intended to remain unstable. If yes, it is a deliberate uncertainty rather than a repair requirement. No Must-Fix findings arise from the text.
