## Pass 0 — Reverse outline

1. Establishes Marley’s death as incontrovertible, making the supernatural premise legible.
2. Establishes Scrooge’s isolation and cruelty through Fred, Bob, and the charity collectors.
3. Marley appears, names Scrooge’s moral danger, and sets the three-spirit deadline.
4. Christmas Past shows the emotional causes of Scrooge’s present condition: abandonment, Fan, Fezziwig’s humane leadership, and Belle’s release.
5. Christmas Present contrasts Scrooge’s wealth with communal joy, the Cratchits’ precarity, Fred’s charity toward him, and the social threats of Ignorance and Want.
6. Christmas Yet To Come shows the social consequences of Scrooge’s unchanged life: indifference, plunder, debtor relief, Tiny Tim’s death, and Scrooge’s unvisited grave.
7. Scrooge accepts responsibility, wakes on Christmas Day, and immediately performs restitution through charity, reconciliation, and changed employment practice.
8. The ending confirms the sustained change and Tiny Tim’s survival.

## Pass 1 — Narrative spine

The central question is clear and sustained: can Scrooge change before the moral future implied by Marley’s chain becomes fixed?

The governing escalation works cleanly:

- Past identifies the wound and the choices that formed him.
- Present identifies the people presently harmed by his values.
- Future makes those values consequential, personal, and irreversible.
- The ending tests change through action, not merely remorse.

## Pass 7 — POV distribution / perspective slips

The narration is third-person omniscient with a highly present, apostrophic narrator. Its dominant emotional attachment is Scrooge: the text repeatedly records what he sees, recognizes, misunderstands, fears, and resolves.

The narration also temporarily enters broad social and comic description, especially at Fezziwig’s, in Christmas Present’s city survey, and at Fred’s party. These are not unmarked POV slips; they belong to the narrator’s established mode from the opening “Mind!” onward.

The Future scenes retain discipline because Scrooge receives them as Spirit-mediated shadows. The final confirmation—“He did it all, and infinitely more”—properly exits the immediate Scrooge-bound viewpoint to close the completed moral arc.

No perspective-slip finding.

## Pass 10 — Entity and timeline check

**Entities**

- Jacob Marley: dead seven years on Christmas Eve; former partner; ghostly warning agent.
- Ebenezer Scrooge: surviving partner, employer of Bob, uncle of Fred, former fiancé of Belle.
- Fred: Scrooge’s nephew and Fan’s son.
- Bob Cratchit: Scrooge’s clerk; husband and father in the Cratchit household.
- Tiny Tim: Bob’s disabled son; future survival depends on altered conditions.
- Belle: Scrooge’s former fiancée; later wife and mother in the Past vision.
- Fezziwig: Scrooge’s former employer and countermodel of humane authority.

**Timeline**

- Christmas Eve afternoon/evening: office scenes, dinner, return home.
- Christmas Eve night: Marley appears.
- Supernaturally compressed night: Past, Present, and Future visitations.
- Christmas Day: Scrooge’s public and familial restitution.
- Next morning: Bob’s lateness becomes the occasion for the salary raise and practical assistance.
- Future visions: explicitly conditional projections, not fixed events.

No unresolved entity, kinship, or temporal contradiction identified.

## Synthesis

This is structurally complete and unusually tight. Its main design strength is recursive reversal: nearly every early cruelty receives a corresponding final action. The manuscript also avoids a purely sentimental conversion by making the change legible across private memory, employment power, family belonging, public charity, and social responsibility.
