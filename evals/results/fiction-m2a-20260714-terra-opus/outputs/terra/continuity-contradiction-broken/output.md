## Pass 0 — Reverse outline

1. Miriam returns to Bellweather after her mother’s death; the estate’s orchard condition and synchronized stopped clocks establish a dispute with Edmund.
2. Their opposing sale/retention positions sharpen the inheritance conflict.
3. Miriam finds account entries tying surveyor Pell to the orchard and notices a missing enclosure.
4. Mrs. Pell explains that Pell’s survey proved railway harm and that the clocks encode a bearing to its hiding place.
5. Miriam follows the bearing to the icehouse; physical evidence implicates Edmund.
6. Edmund admits he took the survey after accepting money from the syndicate; the survey establishes the threatened water damage.
7. Miriam and Edmund disclose the evidence, refuse the sale, and the orchard/wells endure through harvest.

## Pass 1 — Structural diagnosis

The manuscript has a complete, efficient inquiry structure: disturbance → clue → interpretive key → search → confrontation → proof → decision. The stopped clocks, missing enclosure, and mortar dust form a clean escalating evidence sequence. The ending resolves the sale conflict, the survey’s purpose, the clock clue, and Edmund’s role.

Its principal structural weakness is not missing plot architecture but contradictory factual scaffolding. The final chapter explicitly asks the reader to treat chronology as carefully established, while its arithmetic and family facts contradict earlier chapters.

## Pass 5 — Character cards and agency

- **Miriam:** Goal: prevent an uninformed orchard sale and understand her mother’s condition. Method: interprets evidence, seeks Pell’s widow, confronts Edmund, sends the decisive letters. Agency is strong and consistently causal.
- **Edmund:** Goal: sell the orchard and relieve debt. Method: accepts syndicate money, removes the survey, then confesses and cooperates. His reversal is legible because the survey proves concrete harm.
- **Mother:** Posthumous architect of the test. Her will condition and clock code create time and direction for Miriam’s investigation.
- **Mrs. Pell:** Gatekeeper who supplies both the transit-level explanation and the survey’s stakes.

The primary character-card defect is the age contradiction recorded in `F-P5-01`.

## Pass 7 — POV distribution and perspective slips

The narrative remains tightly aligned with Miriam’s investigation. It does not enter Edmund’s or the mother’s interiority except through Edmund’s spoken admission. No material POV slip found.

One phrase has a mild attribution ambiguity: “beginning with her marriage in 1832” in Chapter Three does not identify whether “her” is Miriam or the mother. Chapter Seven appears to rely on that same marriage as a dated fact.

## Pass 8 — Reveal timeline, dropped threads, fairness

No material dropped thread found. The ending pays off the clocks’ “instruction,” the missing enclosure, the orchard condition, and the threatened wells. The timing contradiction in `F-P10-01` weakens the fairness of the final recap rather than the solution itself.

## Synthesis

This is a structurally complete, concise inheritance mystery with a well-integrated central device: the clocks function first as disturbance, then as code, then as proof of the mother’s design. Miriam drives the solution through successive evidence-based choices, and the resolution is proportionate to the established moral and practical stakes.

Two Must-Fix continuity failures are concentrated in the final factual recap: Edmund’s age/birth order and the chronology connecting 1832 to 1841. Correcting those is necessary because the manuscript explicitly frames both as facts being carefully remembered or dated. A Should-Fix causal gap remains around how Edmund located and removed the survey before Miriam’s arrival.
