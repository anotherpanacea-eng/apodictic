## Pass 0 — Reverse outline

1. Miriam returns after her mother’s death; stopped clocks and a will condition frame a mystery around the west orchard.
2. Conflict with Edmund is established: immediate sale versus delayed harvest/inquiry.
3. Miriam finds accounts, Pell’s survey entry, and a removed enclosure.
4. Mrs. Pell explains the survey, its stakes, and the clocks’ directional code.
5. Miriam and Edmund follow the clue to the icehouse; physical traces implicate Edmund.
6. Edmund confesses he concealed the survey in exchange for debt relief; the survey proves harm to tenant water.
7. Miriam acts on the evidence; Edmund joins the refusal, and the orchard is preserved through harvest.

## Pass 1 — Structural premise and escalation

The story has a clean governing question: why did the mother require the orchard to be retained, and what do the stopped clocks mean? Each early chapter narrows that question toward evidence and then a moral choice. Stakes are concrete and proportionate: sale proceeds versus tenant wells and millstream.

## Synthesis

This is a compact, structurally complete mystery with a disciplined evidence chain and a decisive protagonist. Its principal structural vulnerability is not missing plot logic but the under-specified physical status of the clocks: the story gives their meaning, while leaving their synchronized stopping as an unresolved mechanism. The remaining findings are low-impact ambiguities in the mother’s exact intention and the syndicate’s post-refusal pressure.
